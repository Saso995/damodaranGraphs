Intro to Discount Rates

Section description

In this section you can find data useful to estimate the Cost of Equity (risk free rate, corporate tax rate, beta, ERP and CRP) and to evaluate its reliability by comparing your estimate with industry/market averages. This page is meant to serve as a recap of the DCF framework and the theory behind it. You can always find a comprehensive explanation of the topic in the book of A. Damodaran, Investment Valuation.

DCF Approach

Discounted Cash Flows (DCF) models are based on the present value approach: the value of any asset is the present value of the expected future cash flows generated by the asset itself.
The discount rate should reflect the riskiness of the estimated cash flows, thus it varies depending on the model used.

  • n = expected life of the asset
  • CFt = cash flow at time t
  • r = discount rate

FCFE Model

The Free Cash Flow to Equity Model (FCFE) allows to estimate the value of the equity by discounting the expected free cash flow to equity. The appropriate discount rate is the Cost of Equity: the rate of return required by an investor to invest in the equity of a specific company.

  • n = expected life of the asset
  • FCFEt = free cash flow to equity at time t
  • Ke = cost of equity

FCFF Model

The Free Cash Flow to the Firm Model (FCFF) can be used to estimate the value of the firm. The enterprise value (EV) is set equal to the present value of the free cash flow to the firm. According to Modigliani-Miller, owning a firm is the same as holding a portfolio of debt and equity whose expected return is the weighted average of the expected returns on debt and equity, respectively weighted by the fractions of debt and equity in total firm value. In this case, the appropriate discount rate is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).

  • n = expected life of the asset
  • FCFFt = free cash flow to the firm at time t
  • WACC = weighted average cost of capital

How to estimate the discount rate?

As we noted earlier, we use either the Cost of Equity or the WACC, depending on the model. Let's start talking about the WACC, since as we will note later, the cost of equity is one of the term needed for its computation.
The WACC is the weighted average cost of capital:

  • Kd = cost of debt
  • Ke = cost of equity
  • Ks = return on any other security, if any
  • t = corporate income tax rate
  • D = market value of debt
  • E = market value of equity
  • S = market value of any other security, if any

Some of the terms above are directly observable, like the corporate income tax rate; other factors must be estimated. This is the case of the Cost of Equity. One of the most known and widely used model to estimate the cost of equity is the CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model). This model is built around the return that investors can make on riskless investments and the risk premium that they should charge for investing in risky assets. According to the CAPM, the cost of equity is equal to the risk free rate plus the Market Risk Premium. The premium is proportionate to the systematic risk of the asset, captured by the beta. However, a revised version of the model suggests adjusting for country-specific risks by adding the Country Risk Premium:

  • Ke = cost of equity
  • rf = risk free rate
  • β = beta
  • ERP = equity risk premium
  • λ = country risk exposure
  • CRP = country risk premium
