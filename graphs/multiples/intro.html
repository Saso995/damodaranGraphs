Intro to Multiples

Section description

In this section you can different multiples observed in the market. This page is meant to serve as a recap of the relative valuation approach and the theory behind it. You can always find a comprehensive explanation of the topic in the book of A. Damodaran, Investment Valuation.

Relative valuation

In relative valuation, the value of an asset is compared to the values assessed by the market for similar or comparable assets. There are two main building-blocks in relative valuation: standardized prices and comparison. To value a firm on a relative basis, prices must be standardized. This is done by converting prices into multiples of earnings, book values, sales, ecc. Afterwards, conclusions are reached by comparing these multiples with those of similar/comparable firms. However, finding comparable firms is not an easy task since no two firms are identical.

Three steps

Three steps are necessary to do relative valuation:

  • 1) Identify a comparable firm/asset and obtain market values
  • 2) Convert these values into standardized values since the absolute prices cannot be compared
  • 3) Compare the standardized values of the firm/asset under analysis with those of the comparable firms/assets


Stock market multiples vs. Deal multiples

Multiples can refer to values taken from two market context: the stock market (stock market multiples) and the market for corporate control (deal multiples). These two types of multiples usually lead to the estimation of different values. In normal situation, in fact, stock multiple express stand-alone values; deal multiples refer to expected market prices and reflect control premium.

Distintions

Multiples can refer to values taken from two market context: the stock market (stock market multiples) and the market for corporate control (deal multiples). These two types of multiples usually lead to the estimation of different values. In normal situation, in fact, stock multiple express stand-alone values; deal multiples refer to expected market prices and reflect control premium.

Another distinction is between financial multiples and business multiples: financial multiples seek to identify the connection between a company’s equity market price and some important financial measure (net income, sales, etc.). Business (sector-specific) multiples link a company’s equity market price to specific elements which are relevant for the business model typical of the sector the company operates in.

Pros and cons

There are several reasons why relative valuation is so widely used:
  • Simpler and faster than DCF: a relative valution is requires far fewer assumptions than DCF valution and it's less time consuming. Moreover, it is easier to understand and simpler to present to clients/customers.
  • Market-oriented: a relative valuation is able to reflect the current market sentiment, since it is an attempt to measure relative and not intrinsic value
The strengths of relative valuation are also its major weaknesses:
  • Few explicit assumptions: the ease with which a relative valuation can be put together, pulling together (i) a multiple and (ii) a peer group, can also result in inconsistent estimates of value where key variables such as risk, growth or cash flow potential are ignored
  • Market-oriented: The fact that multiples reflect the market mood also implies that using relative valuation to estimate the value of an asset can result in values that are too high when the market is overvaluing comparable firms, or too low when it is undervaluing these firms
  • Simplicity: while there is scope for bias in any type of valuation, the lack of transparency regarding the underlying assumptions in relative valuations makes them particularly vulnerable to manipulation.
