Cost of Capital

Description

The Cost of Capital, also known as WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital), is the return required by a well-diversified investor to hold the stream of future cash flows generated by an asset. According to Modigliani-Miller, buying all the security issued by a company, you end up having a 100% claim on the firmâ€™s assets and thus on the stream of cash flows deriving from them. At the same time, you hold a portfolio of debt and equity whose expected return is the weighted average of the expected returns on debt and equity, respectively weighted by the fractions of debt and equity.

Data explanation

  • Cost of Capital: Cost of Equity * (Equity / (Debt + Equity)) + Cost of Debt * (1 - Marginal tax rate) * (Debt / (Debt + Equity)), with aggregated debt and market equity values across all companies in the sector, using most recent balance sheet for debt and most recent year-end for equity
  • Cost of Equity: Risk free Rate + Beta * Equity Risk Premium
  • Cost of Debt: Pre-tax cost of borrowing for sector, estimated based upon the standard deviation of equity
  • After-tax Cost of Debt: Pre-tax cost of borrowing * (1 - Marginal tax rate)
  • D/(D+E): Total Debt (including lease debt)/ (Total Debt (including lease debt) + Market Cap), aggregated across all firms in group, with all numbers other than market cap coming from most recent balance sheet; market cap is as of last day of the most recent year
  • Beta: it is the simple average across firms of each firm's beta, taken as a weighted average of 2-year and 5-year weekly return regression betas, with 2-year betas weighted 2/3rds. If the company has only a 2-year beta, this one is used
  • Tax rate: average effective tax rate in the most recent 12 months
  • Risk free rate: assumed equal to the Long Term Treasury bond rate (1.92%)
  • Equity Risk Premium: for more details check out Damodaran's excel files
